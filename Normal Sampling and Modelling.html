Normal Sampling and Modelling

Normal sampling and modeling involve selecting a small but representative group (a sample) from a larger population to draw conclusions about the whole. This is especially useful when measuring the entire population isn’t practical. If the population follows a normal distribution—where most values are concentrated around the average, forming a bell-shaped curve—the sample will typically reflect that pattern. By analyzing the sample, we can make reliable predictions about the entire population. The larger the sample, the more accurately it represents the population, improving the reliability of our conclusions.



Although normal distributions often deal with continuous data, discrete data, such as the number of earthquakes that you count in whole numbers, can sometimes be modeled with a normal distribution. Even though it’s not continuous like height or time, if you have a big enough set of this data and it forms a symmetric, bell-shaped pattern with one peak, a statistician might fit a smooth normal curve to it. Once that curve fits, they can use it to make predictions about the data, just like they would with continuous stuff.



Formulas

The Z-Score formula calculates how far a value is from the mean in standard deviations:

\[ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \]

Where:

  • \( z \) is the z-score (standardized value).
  • \( x \) is the observed value (e.g., height).
  • \( \mu \) is the population mean.
  • \( \sigma \) is the population standard deviation.

The Standard Error measures the variability of the sample mean:

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Where:

  • \( SE \) is the standard error.
  • \( \sigma \) is the population standard deviation.
  • \( n \) is the sample size.


Example:

A researcher measures the heights of 50 randomly selected adults and finds the average height is 170 cm with a standard deviation of 10 cm. Assuming the population is normally distributed, what’s the probability that a randomly chosen person from the population is taller than 180 cm?

Given Information are as follow:

  • Mean height: \( \mu = 170 \) cm
  • Standard Deviation: \( \sigma = 10 \) cm
  • Observed value: \( x = 180 \) cm
  • Sample size: 50 (not directly used here, but confirms population normality assumption)

Calculate the Z-Score using the formula:

\[ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \]

Substitute the values:

\[ z = \frac{180 - 170}{10} = \frac{10}{10} = 1 \]



A z-score of 1 means 180 cm is 1 standard deviation above the mean.

Using a standard normal table above, the probability of being less than \( z = 1 \) is approximately 0.8413 (84.13%).

Since we want the probability of being greater than 180 cm, subtract from 1:

\[ P(x > 180) = 1 - 0.8413 = 0.1587 \]

Therefore, the probability that a randomly chosen person is taller than 180 cm is approximately 0.1587, or 15.87%.



A company tests the battery life of 100 phones and gets an average of 20 hours with a standard deviation of 2 hours. If they use a normal model, how would the spread of the sample means change if they tested 400 phones instead?

Show Answer

Given Information are as follow:

  • Mean battery life: \( \mu = 20 \) hours
  • Standard Deviation: \( \sigma = 2 \) hours
  • Initial sample size: \( n_1 = 100 \)
  • New sample size: \( n_2 = 400 \)

Calculate the Standard Error (SE) using the formula:

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

For 100 phones:

\[ SE_1 = \frac{2}{\sqrt{100}} = \frac{2}{10} = 0.2 \text{ hours} \]

For 400 phones:

\[ SE_2 = \frac{2}{\sqrt{400}} = \frac{2}{20} = 0.1 \text{ hours} \]

The spread of the sample means is the standard error, which decreases from 0.2 hours to 0.1 hours.

It just means that, the spread gets halved because a larger sample size reduces the standard error, making the sample mean more precise.

Therefore, the spread of the sample means would decrease from 0.2 hours to 0.1 hours.



A city records the number of daily car accidents over 200 days, with an average of 5 accidents per day and a standard deviation of 2. Since the data is discrete but forms a bell shape, how could a statistician use a normal model to predict the chance of having 8 or more accidents in a day?

Show Answer

What given:

  • Mean accidents: \( \mu = 5 \) per day
  • Standard Deviation: \( \sigma = 2 \)
  • Observed value: \( x = 8 \) accidents
  • Sample size: 200 days (supports normal approximation)

Since the data is discrete but bell-shaped with a large sample, we can approximate it with a normal model as stated earlier.

Calculate the Z-Score for 8 accidents:

\[ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \]

Substitute the values:

\[ z = \frac{8 - 5}{2} = \frac{3}{2} = 1.5 \]

A z-score of 1.5 means 8 accidents is 1.5 standard deviations above the mean.

Using a standard normal table from above, the probability of being less than \( z = 1.5 \) is approximately 0.9332 (93.32%).

For 8 or more accidents, we want the area above 1.5, so:

\[ P(x \geq 8) = 1 - 0.9332 = 0.0668 \]

Therefore, a statistician would use the normal model to estimate about a 6.68% chance of 8 or more accidents in a day.
