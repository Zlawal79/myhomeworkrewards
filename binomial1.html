Binomial Distributions

What Is a Binomial Distribution?

A Binomial Distribution is a type of Probability Distribution that follows these conditions:

  • There are multiple Trials.
  • Each Trial has only two possible outcomes: Success or Failure.
  • The Probability of Success is the same in every Trial.
  • The number of Successes in a fixed number of Trials is the Random Variable.
  • The outcome of one Trial does not affect the outcome of others (Independence).

Probability in a Binomial Distribution:

The Binomial Probability Formula calculates the probability of getting exactly \( x \) Successes in \( n \) Trials:

\[ P(X = x) = \binom{n}{x} p^x q^{n-x} \]

Where:

  • \( P(X = x) \): The Probability of having exactly \( x \) Successes in \( n \) Trials.
  • \( \binom{n}{x} \): The Binomial Coefficient, representing the number of ways to choose \( x \) Successes out of \( n \) Trials.
  • \( n \): The total number of Trials or Experiments.
  • \( x \): The number of Successes we are interested in (a value between 0 and \( n \)).
  • \( p \): The Probability of Success on any single Trial.

Expectation for a Binomial Distribution:

The Expected Number of Successes is given by:

\[ E(X) = np \]

Where:

  • \( E(X) \): The Expected Value, or the average number of Successes over many sets of \( n \) Trials.
  • \( n \): The total number of Trials or Experiments.
  • \( p \): The Probability of Success on any single Trial.


Example: Suppose you roll a fair six-sided die 10 times. What is the Expected Number of times you will roll a 3?

Since the die is fair, the Probability of rolling any specific number (like a 3) is:

\[ \dfrac{1}{6} \]

The Expected Value Formula is:

\[ E(X) = np \]

\[ E(X) = 10 \times \dfrac{1}{6} = 1.67 \]

Thus, the Expected Number of times you will roll a 3 is 1.67.



Lee’s family moves to an area with a different telephone exchange. Their new number has four random digits. Lee’s favorite numbers are prime digits: 2, 3, 5, and 7.

i) Calculate the Probability Distribution for the number of these prime digits.

ii) What is the Expected Number of these prime digits?

Show Answer

First, note that the prime digits between 0 and 9 are 2, 3, 5, and 7 (not 11 as it's not a single digit). There are 10 possible digits (0-9), so the Probability of a digit being prime is 4 out of 10.

The number of prime digits \( X \) follows a Binomial Distribution: \( X \sim \text{Binomial}(n = 4, p = \dfrac{4}{10}) \).

The Probability Mass Function for a Binomial Distribution is:

\[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \]

Where \( n = 4 \), \( p = \dfrac{4}{10} = 0.4 \), and \( k \) is the number of prime digits.

Let’s compute the Probability Distribution for \( k = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 \):

  • For \( X = 0 \):

    \[ P(0) = \binom{4}{0} (0.4)^0 (0.6)^4 = 1 \times 1 \times 0.1296 = 0.1296 \]

  • For \( X = 1 \):

    \[ P(1) = \binom{4}{1} (0.4)^1 (0.6)^3 = 4 \times 0.4 \times 0.216 = 0.3456 \]

  • For \( X = 2 \):

    \[ P(2) = \binom{4}{2} (0.4)^2 (0.6)^2 = 6 \times 0.16 \times 0.36 = 0.3456 \]

  • For \( X = 3 \):

    \[ P(3) = \binom{4}{3} (0.4)^3 (0.6)^1 = 4 \times 0.064 \times 0.6 = 0.1536 \]

  • For \( X = 4 \):

    \[ P(4) = \binom{4}{4} (0.4)^4 (0.6)^0 = 1 \times 0.0256 \times 1 = 0.0256 \]

Let’s find the Expected Value:

\[ E(X) = np \]

\[ E(X) = 4 \times 0.4 = 1.6 \]

So, the Expected Number of prime digits is 1.6.
